History

The Mercedes-Benz W125 Rekordwagen was conceived in the late 1930s during a period of fierce competition to achieve the fastest land speed. The car was purpose-built for high-speed record attempts on Germany's Autobahn. On January 28, 1938, with driver Rudolf Caracciola behind the wheel, the W125 set an astonishing record, reaching a top speed of 432.7 km/h (268.9 mph). This feat solidified Mercedes-Benz’s reputation as a pioneer in automotive engineering.

Technology and Engineering

The W125 Rekordwagen was powered by a 5.6-liter supercharged V12 engine, producing 736 horsepower. The engine featured advanced technologies like dual overhead camshafts and forced induction, which were ahead of its time. Its streamlined, aerodynamic body was crafted specifically for high-speed stability, inspired by wind tunnel testing and aircraft design. This remarkable engineering allowed the W125 to slice through the air with minimal drag, essential for achieving record-breaking speeds.

Legacy and Impact

The Mercedes-Benz W125 Rekordwagen remains an icon of speed and innovation. Its record stood unmatched for nearly 80 years and showcased Mercedes-Benz’s commitment to pushing technological boundaries. The car’s legacy continues to inspire the brand's high-performance vehicles and represents the pinnacle of pre-war automotive engineering.

Specifications

  • Engine: 5.6-liter supercharged V12
  • Power: 736 horsepower
  • Top Speed: 432.7 km/h (268.9 mph)
  • Transmission: 5-speed manual
  • Weight: Approx. 1,100 kg
  • Chassis: Aerodynamic aluminum body on tubular steel frame
  • Tires: Specially designed for high-speed stability
